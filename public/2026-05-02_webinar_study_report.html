Webinar Incrementality Study — Final Report

Generated May 02, 2026 · 4Patriots Marketing Analytics

Headline

Yes, webinars are incremental — every attended webinar adds about $170 in 180-day gross profit per established or veteran customer, beyond what they would have spent anyway. The lift survives strict matching against similar non-attendee customers and stays consistent across two independent methods and 8 sensitivity variants. Three operational moments concentrate the impact: webinars reactivate long-dormant customers (29% return rate vs ~2% for matched controls), they pull first-time attendees back for a second webinar (~$285 additional GPM each), and they convert a meaningful share of cold leads — 14% of cold-lead attendees eventually become paying customers, with conversion roughly doubling when they attend a second webinar. The webinar team should be measured monthly on dormant attendances and second-attendance count — the second-attendance KPI captures both retention lift AND cold-lead conversion. Webinars do overlap with super customers, but the matching analysis controls for that — webinars don't just attract whales, they help create them.

Definitions used in this report

Dormant customer. Hasn't purchased in the last 90 days OR hasn't clicked any Klaviyo email/SMS in the last 30 days, as of webinar registration.

New customer. First purchase less than 1 year ago.

Established customer. First purchase 1 to 5 years ago.

Veteran customer. First purchase 5 or more years ago.

Per-attendee lift. The additional 180-day gross profit a webinar attendee generates compared to a similar non-attendee. Estimated by matching every attendee to the most-similar non-attendees on tenure, recent purchase activity, and Klaviyo engagement.

Reactivation. A dormant customer returning to either purchase or click within 90 days post-webinar.

Methodology — how we estimated the per-attendee lift

Propensity-score matching. For each webinar attendee, we found similar non-attendee customers with comparable purchase history, tenure, and email/SMS engagement. The 180-day GPM difference between attendees and matched non-attendees is the per-attendee lift.

Confidence intervals from 200 bootstrap resamples. Sensitivity tested across multiple match settings; estimates stay tightly clustered.

Main KPIs

Real-time observable · compensation-eligible
  • Dormant attendances (count). Each dormant attendee delivers about $170 in additional 180-day GPM that we wouldn't see otherwise. Suggested monthly target: 478 (12-month median). Stretch: 561 (top quartile). At median performance, this drives roughly $83,014 in incremental GPM per month.
  • Second-attendance count. Each repeat attendee delivers about $285 in additional 180-day GPM compared to a one-time attendee. Suggested monthly target: 28 (12-month median). Stretch: 36. At median performance, this drives roughly $7,673 in incremental GPM per month.

How dormancy and second-attendance are operationally defined

Dormant. A customer is dormant at registration if either: (a) no orders in the last 90 days, or (b) no Klaviyo clicks (post-bot-and-transactional filter) in the last 30 days. Both signals matter; the first is the harder problem, the second is the leading indicator.

Second-attendance. An attendee event qualifies if it is the customer's second-ever attended webinar (lifetime, not within period). One customer can contribute exactly one second-attendance event in their lifetime.

Bonus payout (proposed). Both top quartile vs prior period: 100%. One top, one at-target: 75%. Both at-target: 50%. One below-target: 25%. Both below: 0%.

The KPI in Practice — 12-Month Backtest

If the KPI had been in place for the last 12 months, this is the report the webinar team would have shown each month.

Note on the recent climb in dormant attendances. The team's reach to dormant customers grew from 32 in April 2025 to 1,088 in March 2026 — a 30x increase. Driven by both higher attendance volume and better targeting of dormant audiences.
Sales-side reactivation has declined while Klaviyo-side reactivation has held. This decoupling reflects a known macro headwind affecting purchase rates broadly — customers are still receptive to messaging, but converting to purchase has gotten harder. Not a webinar-program issue.
How the backtest was computed

For each of the last 12 calendar months, every attended webinar event was tagged with: (a) was the customer dormant at registration time, (b) was this their second-ever attendance, (c) did they reactivate (place an order or click Klaviyo) within 30/90 days post-attendance.

Reactivation rates for recent months are right-censored (incomplete observation window) and shown as "—".

The Causal Lift

Per-attendee 180-day GPM lift, from propensity-score matching against same-stratum non-attendees with similar pre-Day-0 features. Two methods agree.

Two independent matching methods — PSM (vs non-attendees) and Test D PSM (vs registered-but-no-show) — give the same answer within $40 across both customer cohorts. Convergence across methods is the strongest evidence the lift is real and not an artifact of any single approach.

We re-ran the matching analysis 8 different ways — varying caliper width, number of matches per attendee, and dropping the heaviest attenders. The estimate barely moves, which means the headline number isn't sensitive to analyst choices.

Why both PSM and Test D PSM are shown

PSM matches treated webinar attendees to never-registered non-attendees with similar pre-Day-0 features. This is the workhorse causal estimate.

Test D PSM matches treated attendees to customers who registered for a webinar but didn't show up. Both groups self-selected through registration; only one consumed the treatment. This is the cleanest causal-shaped comparison in the data.

Both methods agree on the direction and rough magnitude. Sensitivity analysis confirms the estimate doesn't depend on caliper width or match-count choices.

The Super-Customer Hypothesis — Confirmed and Controlled

Treated customers spend hundreds of dollars in the 30 days BEFORE their first webinar. Webinars often follow purchases (likely via post-purchase nurture flows). The matching analysis above already controls for this, so the headline lift is incremental on top of the pre-existing momentum.

Why the trajectory matters and how we account for it

The pre-Day-0 ramp from $0 to ~$1,000 cumulative GPM in the days before attendance is the bias to defeat. Naive matchback (sum of attendee orders post-Day-0) would credit this momentum to the webinar.

Phase 4 PSM controls for it by matching on time-windowed RFM features (orders / GPM in the 30/60/180 days before Day 0) plus Klaviyo engagement. Treated and matched-control customers look identical on observed pre-Day-0 features; the post-Day-0 difference is the incremental lift.

Multi-Attendance Dose Response

Customers attending 6+ webinars generate 4-6x the post-Day-0 GPM of one-time attendees. Direction is real; the second-attendance comparison (Tier 1 KPI) confirms this is partly causal.

Descriptive vs causal interpretation

This chart is descriptive — heavy attenders are different in unobserved ways from one-time attenders. We can't claim causality from the dose-response curve alone.

The Test D PSM analysis (in the Causal Lift section) and the second-attendance PSM in Phase 5 give us the causal confirmation that returning to a second webinar adds ~$285 in 180-day GPM. So the curve isn't pure self-selection.

Do Webinars Work on Cold Leads?

Yes — but slowly, and the second attendance is what makes them stick. About a third of webinar attendees come to a webinar before they've ever bought from us. Most never buy. The ones who do tend to convert after multiple webinars over a multi-month nurture window. This is the same second-attendance pattern we see in paying customers — meaning the 2nd-attendance KPI does double duty: it deepens engagement with existing customers AND it converts cold leads.

35%of webinar attendees were cold leads (attended before any purchase, or never purchased).
14%of cold leads eventually became paying customers (lifetime conversion rate).

The second attendance is the inflection point — even for cold leads. Cold leads who attend just one webinar convert at 11%. Cold leads who attend two or more before any purchase convert at 23-43%. This mirrors the existing KPI 2 finding for paying customers ($285 incremental GPM per second attendance). The 2nd-attendance KPI deserves even more emphasis: it captures both retention lift AND cold-lead conversion in the same metric.
Lead time, post-conversion LTV, and acquisition channel

Cold-lead funnel is slow. Median 155 days from first webinar attendance to first purchase. The middle 50% spans 38-300 days. Plan a multi-month nurture window for cold-lead attendees — a one-touch follow-up will miss most of them.

Conversion timing. 3% within 30 days, 6% within 90 days, 13% within a year. The seed gets planted at the webinar; the harvest takes months.

Post-conversion LTV is real. Mean 180-day GPM of $1,386–$1,515 for converted cold leads acquired in 2023-2024. 2025 dropped to $868 (consistent with the macro headwind affecting all sales-side reactivation rates). 2026 cohort is right-censored (still in their 180-day window).

Conversion mechanism is list-based, not retargeting. 56% of converted cold leads have House Email, House SMS, or Unknown as their top sales channel. Suggests the conversion path is "attended → got on the email/SMS list → got nurtured → bought." Paid retargeting accounts for a small share. This points to capturing cold-lead emails at registration as a high-leverage operational improvement.

Methodology and one bias we caught

Cold lead definition. An attendee whose first webinar attendance pre-dates their first purchase, OR an attendee with no purchase history at all (the never-customer cohort that's long been understood as the $0-LTV outlier).

Survivor bias fix. The first version of this analysis used lifetime attendance count to bucket cold leads — but converted cold leads keep attending after they buy, which inflated the high-attendance buckets artificially. The chart above uses attendances STRICTLY BEFORE first purchase, so the dose-response is the genuine pre-conversion signal.

What we did NOT do here. This is descriptive — comparing converted-cold-lead 180-day LTV against same-year first-time customers acquired through other channels would tell us whether the cold-lead funnel attracts higher- or lower-quality customers. That benchmark is still open.

Dormant Reactivation — The Strongest Single Effect

This chart uses sales-only dormancy: a customer is "dormant" here if they have not placed an order in 90+ days at the time of webinar registration (not the dual sales-OR-Klaviyo definition used in the operational KPI). Attendees reactivate (place an order within 180 days) at 28–30%. Matched non-attendee dormant customers reactivate at 1.5–3.6%. Lift of 26+ percentage points.

Why this is the headline — and why we elevated it as the primary KPI

Reactivation is operationally the hardest customer-marketing problem. Most retention tactics produce single-digit-percentage-point lift on dormant customers. A 26+pp lift is exceptional.

The metric is also real-time observable: at registration, the team can check whether the customer was dormant and tag the attendance accordingly. This makes it a clean KPI for monthly reporting and bonus calibration.

The Substitution Effect (Surprising Finding)

Within low-AOV established customers, attendees with NO Klaviyo activity in the 30 days before registration respond 3-4x more than attendees with high Klaviyo activity. Webinars appear to be a substitute brand-engagement channel, most valuable for off-list customers.

Strategic implication. Webinar promotion to email/SMS lists may be saturated. The largest under-tapped lever appears to be reaching customers who are NOT on the engagement list — direct mail invitations, retargeting ads, post-purchase invitation flows. This is why "non-Klaviyo-engaged attendances" is a Tier 2 KPI.
Caveat: this is one segment cut, not a controlled comparison

This finding is descriptive within the treated population. It compares average outcomes across Klaviyo activity buckets without a counterfactual. The pattern is suggestive but not a causal claim that a "non-Klaviyo-engaged" targeting strategy would actually outperform.

Phase 7 (lift study) would be the right place to validate this with randomized targeting.

Channel Patterns (Hypothesis-Generating)

Customer acquisition channel and engagement channel correlate with webinar response. Facebook-acquired customers consistently respond less; organic / loyalty / House Email-engaged customers respond more.

Important methodological note

This is descriptive only. The CATE differences across channels are not controlled for tenure, RFM, or other observable confounders. Channels also differ in attendance count and audience composition.

The pattern is real enough to motivate hypothesis: Facebook customers are more transactional and less brand-loyal, and webinars work best on already-engaged segments. Confirming this requires a within-channel propensity-matched analysis, which is logged for follow-up.

Treatment Effect Heterogeneity

The mean lift hides a wide distribution. Some attendees benefit a lot. The bottom 10% see near-zero or slightly negative effect. The top 10% see hundreds of dollars more than the average.

How to use this

The causal forest model produces a per-customer estimated treatment effect (CATE). We can use these to identify high-value segments to target and low-value segments to deprioritize.

The KPI framework partly captures this: dormant attendances and second-attendances both correlate with high CATE. Phase 7 lift study will validate the segment-level targeting strategy.

Suggested Growth Tactics

Tactical follow-ups derived from the findings above. Each tactic targets a specific high-leverage moment the analysis surfaced.

  1. Postcard / direct mail to dormant + off-list customers. Customers dormant on both signals (90+ days no purchase AND 30+ days no Klaviyo clicks) are the audience with the strongest webinar response (substitution effect). Email and SMS won't reach them by definition. A postcard test to a sample of this segment, measured against a holdout, would validate the off-list lever.
  2. Triggered second-attendance flow for first-time attendees. Each second attendance generates ~$285 in additional 180-day GPM. A short Klaviyo flow (3-5 touches over 30-60 days post-first-attendance, themed around "if you liked X, you'll like Y") could meaningfully raise second-attendance count without new acquisition spend.
  3. Whale invitation flow tied to dormancy onset. When a top-quintile-GPM customer hits 60-day sales dormancy, trigger a personal-feeling webinar invitation (priority seating, executive Q&A, etc.). Combines two strong signals — high-value customer + dormancy starting — and would test the upper bound of per-attendee value.
  4. Post-purchase nurture flow expansion. The pre-Day-0 trajectory shows webinars often follow purchases via existing nurture flows. Adding a second webinar invite later in the post-purchase journey (60-90 days post-purchase, when first-purchase momentum is fading) could capture customers we currently miss.
  5. Outbound phone calls — focused leverage points. Phone has the highest cost per touch, so target where the marginal value is highest:
    • Registered-but-no-show customers — they self-selected interest and didn't convert. Reminder/recovery call could be high-leverage. The Test D analysis (above) shows these customers respond when they do attend.
    • Recently-dormant veteran customers — high lifetime value already established, dormancy onset signals churn risk.
    • Avoid dialing fully cold or non-customers — the data shows webinars don't convert non-customers.
  6. Off-list paid retargeting. Run paid social or display retargeting to past customers who are NOT on the Klaviyo list, inviting them to the next webinar. Same off-list logic as the postcard tactic, but at scale via existing retargeting infrastructure.
  7. Cold-lead nurture flow with re-invitation. 35% of attendees are cold leads, and a second webinar attendance roughly doubles their conversion rate (11% → 23%+). Build a multi-month nurture flow for first-time cold-lead attendees that re-invites them to subsequent webinars. Median converter lead time is 155 days, so plan for a 6-month nurture window, not a 30-day follow-up sequence. This is the second-attendance flow (tactic 2 above) extended to non-customers.

All tactics are priors to be validated. Phase 7 lift study would be the right place to formally test the highest-priority ones.

What's Next

  • Adopt the 2×2 KPI framework for monthly reporting and bonus calibration. Backtest shows the metric is operationally readable and moves month-over-month with meaningful magnitude.
  • Test the substitution effect. Run a structured pilot promoting webinars to off-list customer audiences (direct mail, retargeting). Compare to standard email promotion.
  • Phase 7 — Lift study design. Randomized webinar-invite holdout test for definitive causal validation. Closes the only remaining methodological caveat (conditional ignorability).
  • Open follow-up questions worth investigating later:
    • Compare webinar response across acquisition channels (Facebook, House Email, etc.) after controlling for other customer differences. Early signal: Facebook-acquired customers respond less than email-engaged customers — but this might just be tenure or RFM differences in disguise.
    • Build a customer-by-customer model that estimates each repeat attendee's individual lift. Today we report an average; per-customer estimates would help pinpoint who specifically benefits most from a second webinar.
    • Test whether different webinar topics or formats (educational sessions vs product walkthroughs) drive different responses. Worth doing once we have enough sample per topic.
    • Run a controlled experiment to validate the substitution-effect finding — does promoting webinars to off-list customers actually pay off? See Phase 7 lift study.
